# Complete Pharmacovigilance Assessment Report

**Role Simulated:** Drug Safety Associate
**Case Type:** Individual Case Safety Report (ICSR)

---

## 1. Case Narrative

A 32-year-old male patient, **Ramesh Patel**, weighing 65 kg, presented with **Acute Bacterial Sinusitis** and had no known history of drug allergy and no family history of diabetes or hypertension. He was prescribed **Tab. Amoxicillin 500 mg TID for 5 days**, started on **1st March 2026**.

On **3rd March 2026** (Day 3 of therapy), the patient developed **generalized itching and skin rashes over the chest**. Amoxicillin was discontinued the same day, and the patient was treated with **antihistaminics**. He recovered completely by **6th March 2026**.

---

## 2. Step 1 — ADR Identification

| Element | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Suspected drug** | Amoxicillin 500 mg TID |
| **Adverse event identified** | Generalized itching and skin rash (chest) |
| **Signal type** | Cutaneous hypersensitivity reaction — consistent with a known class effect of beta-lactam antibiotics |
| **Confounders checked** | No concomitant medications reported other than antihistaminics started *after* reaction onset; no other new exposures (food, other drugs) noted |
| **Conclusion** | Event qualifies as a suspected Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR) requiring formal reporting |

## 4. Step 3 — Causality Assessment

### (a) WHO-UMC Criteria

| Criterion | Finding | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Temporal relationship | Reaction occurred 2 days after starting drug — plausible | Positive |
| Dechallenge | Reaction resolved after stopping drug + antihistaminic treatment | Positive |
| Alternative causes | No other new drug, food, or illness identified | None identified |
| Rechallenge | Not performed | Not assessable |

Since rechallenge was not performed, "Certain" cannot be assigned. All other criteria are positive with no alternative explanation → **classified as Probable**.

### (b) Naranjo Algorithm (Quantitative Cross-Check)

| # | Question | Answer | Score |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Previous conclusive reports of this reaction? | Yes (known beta-lactam hypersensitivity) | +1 |
| 2 | Did AE appear after the suspected drug? | Yes | +2 |
| 3 | Did reaction improve on discontinuation/antagonist? | Yes | +1 |
| 4 | Did AE reappear on rechallenge? | Unknown | 0 |
| 5 | Alternative causes present? | No | +2 |
| 6 | Reaction reappear with placebo? | Unknown | 0 |
| 7 | Drug detected in body fluids at toxic levels? | Not tested | 0 |
| 8 | Dose-response relationship observed? | Not applicable | 0 |
| 9 | Similar reaction to same/similar drug previously? | No prior history | 0 |
| 10 | Confirmed by objective evidence? | Yes (visible rash on exam) | +1 |

**Total Score = 7 → Probable ADR**

Both the qualitative WHO-UMC assessment and the quantitative Naranjo score converge on the same classification, strengthening confidence in the causality conclusion.

## 5. Step 4 — Severity / Seriousness & Expectedness Classification

| Element | Assessment | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Seriousness** | **Non-Serious** | Did not result in death, was not life-threatening, did not require/prolong hospitalization, caused no persistent disability, and was not medically important beyond standard outpatient management |
| **Expectedness** | **Expected** | Skin rash and pruritus are well-documented, labeled ADRs of Amoxicillin and the penicillin class |
| **Severity grade** | **Mild–Moderate** | Symptomatic, resolved fully with supportive treatment (antihistaminics) within 3 days of onset, no sequelae |

**Reporting priority:** As a Non-Serious, Expected ADR, this case would typically be captured through **routine pharmacovigilance channels** (e.g., inclusion in the next Periodic Safety Update Report/PSUR) rather than requiring expedited 15-day regulatory reporting — which is reserved for Serious and/or Unexpected events.
